This section assumes a solid understanding of Kirchhoff's Voltage Law, also known as KVL, (The sum of voltages around a loop is zero).

Mesh Analysis

Mesh analysis is used to calculate currents when they are flowing in loops of a circuit. There are 4 steps to follow.

  1. Assign a current in a clockwise direction for each loop.
    N.B. Current could be assigned counter-clockwise instead of clockwise. As long as the direction used is consistent across the circuit, there will be no issues - it is just most conventional to go clockwise.
  2. Indicate the polarity of voltage across each component, considering the direction of the current.
  3. Apply KVL for each loop. The polarity of voltage sources will not be affected by the direction of assigned currents - they stay as designated originally.
  4. Solve the resultant simultaneous equations for the loop currents.



Loop One      Loop Two
$+ E{_1} - V{_1} - V{_3} = 0$   $- V{_3} - V{_2} + E{_2} = 0$
$2 - 2I{_1} - 4(I{_1} - I{_2}) = 0$   $-6(I{_2}-I{_1}) - I{_2} - 6= 0$
$- 6I{_1} + 4I{_2} = -2$   $4I{_1} - 5I{_2} = 6$
With some simple equation manipulation (not shown here - you can do it yourself!) it is possible to get the answer:
$I{_1} = -1A$   $I{_2} = -2A$


This equation was simple. If you have more variables however, your ability to solve simultaneous equations with matrices can save a lot of time and make things much easier.
For a connected network containing b branches and n nodes, there will be $l = b - n +1$ equations of the form:

$e_1^0 = R_{11}i_1 + R_{12}i_2 + \dots + R_{1l}i_l$
$e_2^0 = R_{21}i_1 + R_{22}i_2 + \dots + R_{2l}i_l$
$\dots  \dots  \dots  \dots  \dots  \dots$
$e_l^0 = R_{l1}i_1 + R_{l2}i_2 + \dots + R_{ll}i_l$ 		  OR   $\begin{bmatrix} e_1^0 \\e_2^0 \\ \vdots \\e_l^0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}R_{11} & \dots & R_{1l} \\ R_{21} & \dots & R_{2l} \\ \dots & \dots & \dots \\ R_{l1} & \dots & R_{ll}\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}i_{1} \\ i_{2} \\ \vdots \\ i_{l}\end{bmatrix}$

Where:
$e_1^0 =$ the net source voltage tending to force current in the reference direction around loop $k$
$R_{il} =$ total series resistance in loop $i$
$R_{ij} =$ total series resistance "shared" by loops $i$ and $j$ $(= R_{ji})$

If you are faced with a scenario where you have a current source, use NODAL analysis instead. Although the method for Nodal Analysis is not covered here, it is still possible to use Cramer's Rule to assist your calculations.
